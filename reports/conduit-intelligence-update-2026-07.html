The headline finding

Conduit's public narrative and its underlying digital signal are moving in opposite directions. Product announcements accelerated through H1 2026 — SOC 2 Type II, a new "Promptable Money Movement" push, the Yuno and Onafriq partnerships — while site engagement, organic search demand, and hiring activity all contracted over the same window. This is the single most useful thing in this update for how LaFinteca should read Conduit's actual momentum, separate from its press momentum.

Technology & product architecture

Sourced from Conduit's trust center (trust.conduitpay.com), developer docs (docs.conduit.financial), and engineering LinkedIn profiles — the layers the marketing site doesn't show.

Custody & settlement

Fireblocks (custody) USDC — Ethereum, Base, Solana, Polygon USDT — Ethereum, Tron RLUSD — Ripple Ledger

Fireblocks is the core custody and transaction-execution layer — MPC key management, HSM-grade, policy-controlled. This is the single most important disclosed fact in the trust center: Conduit doesn't self-custody digital assets, it outsources that risk to a named institutional provider.

Infrastructure subprocessors

AWS (cloud) Modern Treasury (ledger) Auth0 (authentication) Google Workspace

Full subprocessor list is five vendors, all disclosed. Modern Treasury handling the ledger layer is notable — it means Conduit is not running money-movement reconciliation on custom-built infrastructure.

Compliance vendor gap

No dedicated KYC/AML vendor appears anywhere in the disclosed subprocessor list. Combined with docs-site language about an in-house KYB/KYC framework and job-profile signals ("building AI-native AML, KYC & Fraud"), this points to a largely in-house compliance build rather than a licensed Sumsub/Persona/Chainalysis-style stack. One individual profile references TRM Labs for blockchain analytics, but that's a personal certification signal, not an official disclosure — flagged as unconfirmed.

Payment rails & fiat corridors

PIX (Brazil) SPEI (Mexico) TED (Brazil) COP payout-only (Colombia) Fedwire FedNow RTP SWIFT SEPA / SEPA Instant RTGS (UK)

Fiat corridors confirmed on the docs site: USD, BRL, MXN, COP (payout-only), EUR, GBP. The COP payout-only distinction matters — Conduit can send to Colombia but the docs don't show the same bidirectional support they have for BRL and MXN. That's a real gap in their LATAM story worth knowing before it comes up in a competitive conversation.

Banking network, count vs. names

Conduit's May 2025 Series A announcement stated over 20 banks in nine countries, including the US, Mexico, Brazil, Nigeria, and Kenya. Their 2026 sales deck updates this to 8 US banks and 22 international banks, 30 total (detail in Section 03). No individual partner bank is named anywhere across the trust center, docs, press materials, or sales deck. The count and geography grew and got more specific over the year; the policy of not naming any bank did not change.

Engineering job-history signals (from individual profiles, since the careers page is currently empty): TypeScript/JavaScript and Python/Django/React appear across the visible engineering team, consistent with the AWS + Fireblocks stack disclosed above.

Stale positioning, worth watching

Both Bridge and BVNK have already been acquired. Stripe closed Bridge in February 2025. Mastercard closed BVNK in March 2026, months before this deck's date. Conduit is still pitching against them as standalone PSPs in current sales collateral. Either the deck has not been refreshed since the acquisitions, or Conduit is deliberately still competing against Bridge and BVNK as embedded products inside Stripe and Mastercard. Worth a follow up check next time this deck is refreshed, and worth noting if it comes up in a prospect conversation: Conduit's own materials have not caught up with how consolidated this category already is.

Product capabilities, in more detail

The deck confirms the two-product structure already noted (Global USD API for developers, No-Code Treasury Dashboard for finance teams), and adds real detail on the API shape and the FX book.

USD ↔ USDT/C USD ↔ BRL USD ↔ MXN USD ↔ EUR USD ↔ GBP USD ↔ HKD 50+ additional currencies

Pay-in rails run wider than pay-out: FedNow, Fedwire, SWIFT, SPEI, PIX, SEPA and SEPA Instant, and FPS/CHAPS for GBP all appear on the pay-in side. On pay-out, SEPA Instant narrows to standard SEPA outbound, and FPS/CHAPS does not appear at all. That reads as a real asymmetry rather than an omission, since the deck is otherwise careful to list rails per direction. Worth confirming directly if a GBP payout use case ever comes up against them.

The sample API call shown is a plain POST /v1/accounts with currency, owner country, a rails array, and a stablecoin boolean, returning 201 in 412ms. Simple, REST, nothing unusual. "After-hours rates after 5pm EST" is called out specifically as a differentiator, implying most competitors' FX pricing goes stale outside US banking hours.

Who they are actually selling to

Three named ICPs: developers and platforms embedding financial services without becoming an FI, fintechs and PSPs needing USD access in emerging markets, and banks, pitched as "your modern correspondent banking alternative." The bank ICP is worth registering. Conduit is not only selling to fintechs anymore. It is pitching itself as infrastructure for licensed institutions too, which is the same customer profile LaFinteca itself sits in. Not a direct threat today, but a signal of where they intend to expand.

Coverage claim

"100+ countries," tiered into supported, supported with enhanced due diligence, and not supported. The map shows the Americas and most of Africa as supported, a band across the Middle East and parts of Eastern Europe as enhanced due diligence, and Russia and large parts of Central Asia as not supported. Consistent with a standard risk-based approach for a FinCEN and FINTRAC registered MSB, nothing surprising here.

Compliance program structure

The deck adds a formal five-pillar BSA/AML program: internal controls, a named BSA/AML officer function, training, independent testing, and ongoing due diligence, run as a continuous cycle. This is the structure of the compliance program, not the vendor stack. It does not resolve the compliance-vendor gap already flagged; it confirms the program is built to a standard MSB framework rather than an ad hoc process.

Clients, expanded

The deck names sixteen client logos, more than we had confirmed from public sources: Meow, Interswitch, PPRO, Rain, Mesh, Onafriq, Waza, Takenos, Fintoc, Travelex, Moniepoint, Fondeadora, Astropay, TapTap Send, Mercado Bitcoin, and Grey. Geography is stated directly as US, Mexico, Brazil, Kenya, Philippines, Argentina, Egypt, South Africa, and Indonesia, "and more." PPRO and Travelex are larger, more established names than the earlier startup-heavy client picture suggested. Treat this as a self-reported logo list rather than a confirmed integration depth. A logo on a sales deck can mean a live enterprise contract or an early pilot.

Investors, expanded

Beyond the Series A participant list already confirmed (Dragonfly, Altos, Sound Ventures, Commerce Ventures, Circle Ventures, Helios, Portage), the deck's investor wall adds Coinbase, Jump Crypto, Inovia, Fin Venture Capital, and Gradient Ventures, which is Google's AI-focused fund. The deck states over $50 million raised total, close to the $53 million figure already on file. Gradient Ventures' presence is worth noting alongside the "Promptable Money Movement" and AI-first developer positioning already flagged. It suggests the agent-payments angle has real strategic backing behind it, not just marketing language.

Paid search and paid social both round to effectively zero across the twelve-month aggregate — this is not a demand-gen-led motion, it's relationship and content-led, consistent with an enterprise BD sales model. That said, keyword-level data shows at least one live Google Ads campaign targeting Spanish-language "cross border payments" queries out of Colombia, so there's a small, recent paid test running underneath an aggregate number that reads as zero.

Data reconciliation flag

Similarweb's page-level branded/non-branded split reports 18% branded / 82% non-branded. But summing actual traffic share across Conduit's full keyword list tells a different story: 78% of all keyword-attributed search traffic goes to terms that are the brand name itself or direct variants/misspellings of it ("conduit," "conduitpay," "conduit crypto," "conduit financial," "cnduit," "condoit," and so on). The single term "conduit" alone accounts for 33.6% of keyword-driven traffic on its own. These two numbers don't reconcile, and it's not our place to guess which methodology Similarweb weighted differently — we're flagging it rather than picking a side. What both cuts agree on: genuine category-intent traffic (things like "b2b stablecoin payments," "cross border payments api") is thin. The content that does rank for non-branded terms is almost entirely top-of-funnel educational content — their SWIFT guide, ACH guide, an M-Pesa explainer, a CNY-vs-CNH blog post — not commercial-intent pages.

Referral sources

Top referrers by share: soundventures.com (50.9%) and app.synaptic.com (41.6%) dominate, followed by businesswire.com (5.6%) and a Wise/TransferWise referral (1.8%, new in May 2026). Sound Ventures is a Series A participant — this is very likely their own portfolio page linking back to Conduit, not organic press pickup. 92.5% of all referral traffic comes from sites Similarweb categorizes as Investing, versus 7.5% from Finance-Other. In plain terms: almost nobody is linking to Conduit except their own investors and a wire service. That's thin for a company positioning itself as category-defining infrastructure.

Hiring signal — corroborates the Chrome finding

The outgoing-traffic data independently confirms what the live browsing pass found on BambooHR. jobs.lever.co was Conduit's dominant outbound link destination for most of the twelve-month window — as high as 97.5% of outbound clicks in January 2026 — then disappears from the data entirely after March 2026. Technographic detection shows the Lever integration itself was formally removed from the site on April 23, 2026. Combined with zero open roles on their current careers page, this is a well-triangulated signal that hiring has genuinely stalled since Q2 2026, not just that a job board link broke.

Similar-sites competitive set (Similarweb's own clustering)

Useful as a sanity check on who Similarweb's algorithm considers Conduit's peers, independent of our own analysis: Marqeta, Dwolla, Finix, Wise-Pay, Payset, Dsgpay, Uniteller, Veloremit, Inpay, Bottomline, Onbe, and several smaller remittance/exchange operators. Notably, neither Bridge nor BVNK appear — both were absorbed into Stripe and Mastercard respectively and no longer register as standalone entities in this kind of clustering, which is itself a reminder of where this category's winners end up.

Technology stack changes on the marketing site (last 12 months)

Two stack changes stand out from the technographic scan. First, a full consumer-payment-method integration set — Apple Pay, Google Pay, PayPal, Venmo, Boleto, dLocal, Mercado Pago, OXXO, iDEAL, UnionPay — was live from January to October 2025, then entirely removed on October 31, 2025. That's a lot of checkout infrastructure to install and then fully strip out; it reads like a demo/self-serve experiment that didn't survive. Second, the site's marketing-analytics stack was significantly cleaned up in May–June 2026 — Hotjar, Clearbit, Marketo, and several ad-tech tags (Beeswax, ClickCertain) were all removed within weeks of each other, consistent with the visible mid-rebrand state we flagged in the original brief (live Lorem ipsum placeholders on production pages).

05

What this changes for LaFinteca

Read this way, not as weakness-hunting

None of this means Conduit is struggling. A relationship-sold infrastructure company can have declining top-of-funnel traffic and stalled marketing-site hiring while its actual transaction volume keeps growing — those aren't the same signal. What it does mean is that the press cadence (SOC 2, partnerships, "Promptable Money Movement") is not currently backed by growing public demand signal, and that's a genuinely useful thing to know before anyone on the team treats Conduit's announcements as evidence of unstoppable momentum in a competitive conversation.

  • Compliance-to-compliance framing just got a named counterpart. Mark Graves (CLO/CCO, CAMS, CRCP) uses almost identical language to LaFinteca's own positioning — "bank-ready, regulation-first." If Sergii or Rafa's team ever ends up in a shared RFP or partner conversation involving Conduit, Graves is the person whose public statements are worth tracking for how they'll defend the banking-partner-network story against a BCB-licensed argument.
  • LATAM is not where Conduit's organization is currently weighted. No titled LATAM BD head, no LATAM GM equivalent to Wainaina's Africa role, and traffic geography confirms Africa outweighs Brazil in their own digital footprint. This is a gap, not a strength — worth testing directly in outbound messaging to Brazil/Mexico prospects who might otherwise assume Conduit has the same regional depth in LATAM that they claim to have in Africa.
  • The COP payout-only limitation is a specific, checkable claim. If a Colombia prospect is evaluating Conduit, ask directly whether they support bidirectional COP flows — the docs currently suggest they don't.
  • Named banking partners remain a closed door. Don't spend further research cycles trying to identify Conduit's eight US banks or thirty total partners. Multiple source types (trust center, docs, press, and now the sales deck) all disclose count and geography but withhold names. This appears to be a deliberate disclosure policy, not a research gap.
  • Conduit's own competitive framing is out of date. Their sales deck still pitches against Bridge and BVNK as standalone PSPs, months after both were absorbed into Stripe and Mastercard. If Bridge or BVNK ever come up in a prospect conversation involving Conduit, that gap is worth pointing out. It suggests their competitive intelligence process is slower than the market it is tracking.
  • Conduit is starting to pitch banks, not just fintechs. Their third named ICP is licensed institutions, framed as a correspondent banking replacement. That is the same customer profile LaFinteca sits in. Not an immediate overlap, but worth a check-in next quarter to see if that ICP expansion turns into actual bank-side client wins.
