Bottom line

Retail prediction markets are a hard no-go for the Brazilian PI entity. The only defensible openings are the CVM/CFTC-regulated lane, adjacent B2B servicing, and a ring-fenced Mexico play. Everything else risks the license.

Two regulatory philosophies are now colliding. Brazil, Colombia, Argentina and Peru read event contracts through gambling law and have moved to block, prosecute or license them out of reach. The US reads the same product through commodities law and is building a federal framework around it, with ICE, Nasdaq, CME and Schwab already entering. That split defines both the risk and the opportunity for a cross-border PSP: the product is booming, but the boom lives almost entirely inside the US regulatory perimeter.

Brazil: banned at retail, open at the exchange.

Two tracks now exist. One is criminalized. The other runs through CVM and B3.

On April 24, 2026, the National Monetary Council issued Resolution 5,298 (in force May 4), expressly prohibiting derivatives whose underlying is a sports, gaming, political, electoral, social, cultural or entertainment event that CVM does not recognize as a genuine economic-financial benchmark. A joint SPA Technical Note (2958/2026/MF) framed retail prediction markets as overlapping with illegal fixed-odds betting under Law 14.790/2023. Anatel blocked 27+ platforms including Polymarket and Kalshi's global product, on top of roughly 39,000 illegal betting domains blocked since January 2025. Licensed betting operators, each of whom paid over R$30m for a license, had formally petitioned the SPA to act.

The securities lane is real and growing. CVM authorized B3 in February 2026 to list binary event contracts on the dollar, the Ibovespa and Bitcoin for professional investors (R$10m+ in assets). Kalshi went live via XP International and Clear on March 9, 2026, offering contracts on Brazilian economic indicators through international investment accounts, structured as CFTC-regulated derivatives accessed offshore. The Finance Ministry confirmed in March it is still studying the SPA-versus-CVM jurisdiction question, so the boundary between the two tracks remains at regulators' discretion.

For LaFinteca's PI entity the exposure stacks three ways. Processing pay-ins or pay-outs for a blocked operator means facilitating an activity the Finance Ministry has declared illegal. License risk Independently of the gambling question, Resolution 561 closes the stablecoin-settlement route a Polymarket-style flow would need. Rail risk And the government has wrapped the whole crackdown in consumer-protection and public-health framing, with a national self-exclusion system logging 217,000+ requests in its first 40 days, an October general election, and a home-soil World Cup summer. Reputational risk Any brand associated with blocked platforms in 2026 Brazil is on the wrong side of the news cycle.

Risk flag

Even a technically indirect flow, such as on-ramping BRL to USDC that funds a blocked platform, puts the PI license and BCB standing at risk. The Brazilian entity must decline this vertical categorically.

Colombia moved first. Coljuegos declared Polymarket illegal and ordered ISP blocks after Fecoljuegos petitioned, citing missing age verification and electoral-integrity risk around the 2026 presidential race. Only 14 licensed operators exist under the monopoly framework and political betting sits entirely outside it. Argentina followed in March: Judge Susana Parada ordered ENACOM to block Polymarket nationwide and directed Apple and Google to pull the apps, classifying it as a concealed online betting system under Article 301 bis. The trigger was a suspicious volume surge of roughly $91,000 on the February CPI contract minutes before the INDEC release. The lesson travels: Argentina is the most crypto-friendly major market in the region, with ~20% population adoption and banks cleared for crypto custody from April 2026, and none of that protected the platform from gambling law.

Peru requires MINCETUR licensing, local presence, .bet.pe domains and a 12% GGR tax, bans crypto for gambling, and has directed payment institutions to block flows to illegal operators. Chile has no authorizing law, which makes online platforms technically illicit; the pending Senate bill would create the SCJ superintendency with payment-blocking powers, though its near-triple regional tax burden keeps final passage in doubt. Mexico is the outlier: Polymarket is freely accessible, the 1947 gaming law never contemplated decentralized markets, and the Fintech Law regulates VASPs but not event contracts. Users on-ramp via Bitso and SPEI into USDC today.

Watch item

Mexico's gambling reform, expected 2025–2026 and partly World Cup-driven, is the single event most likely to close the region's only grey zone. Any Mexico play must be gated on it.

US and Europe: two opposite verdicts on the same product.

The CFTC is building a permissive federal framework. ESMA just declared the product already banned for EU retail.

In the US, event contracts are derivatives under the Commodity Exchange Act. Kalshi won its 2024 district-court challenge on election contracts and the CFTC dropped its appeal in May 2025. Under Chairman Michael Selig the agency has sued roughly ten states to defend exclusive federal jurisdiction, producing a circuit split (Third Circuit for Kalshi against New Jersey, Ninth Circuit allowing Nevada to proceed, Ohio and New York ruling against Kalshi) that points toward the Supreme Court. On June 10, 2026 the CFTC issued a 267-page proposed rule, comments due July 27, that would permit aggregate-outcome sports contracts while banning discrete-play, injury, officiating and pre-collegiate contracts.

Europe went the other way. ESMA's July 3, 2026 statement held that event contracts qualifying as MiFID II financial instruments carry binary payouts and therefore fall under the binary-options product intervention in force since 2018, meaning marketing, distribution and sale to EU retail is already prohibited without any new law. Servicing even non-retail clients requires MiFID II authorization. Tokenized contracts may fall under MiCA or national gambling law; nine national gambling regulators ran a joint World Cup initiative and Spain temporarily blocked both Kalshi and Polymarket in May. Europe has zero licensed prediction-market operators, and the Commission's MiCA-review consultation on prediction contracts runs to September 30, 2026.

Structural read

If the CFTC rule is finalized and federal preemption holds, the "regulated derivative" framing gains a durable template that could pull LatAm securities regulators toward the CVM model over a two-to-three-year horizon. That is the scenario worth positioning for quietly.

Whatever happens in LatAm, this vertical is being absorbed into US capital markets, and that is where its payments spend will concentrate.

The de facto LatAm funding path is a workaround: buy USDC or USDT with Pix in Brazil or SPEI in Mexico on Binance, Mercado Bitcoin, Foxbit or Bitso, bridge to Polygon, fund the platform. Circle's USDC is live from BRL and MXN via national payment systems, and Circle Payments Network (mainnet May 2025) has LatAm off-ramp partners in Alfred Pay, RedotPay and Conduit, though no end-to-end Pix-to-Polymarket flow is announced. Bitso, with its BRL1 stablecoin and Pix/SPEI B2B rails, is the most institutionally relevant regional player and names no prediction-market client. The ramp market itself is consolidating: Wyre shut down, Banxa was acquired by OSL in January 2026, MoonPay is on an acquisition run, and bank de-risking of crypto firms remains the fragile link in every chain.

Signal

The vertical's payments spend flows to MoonPay, Transak, Zero Hash and Circle, none of which are LatAm specialists. The infrastructure layer, and not merchant acquiring, is where the money actually sits.

Competitor posture: the incumbents are staying away.

No major LatAm PSP has planted a flag. That is both a warning and a whitespace.

dLocal EBANX Nuvei TransFi show no public linkage to prediction-market operators or their on-ramp partners. dLocal has joined Circle Payments Network for stablecoin payouts and runs crypto-to-local-rails products; Nuvei operates a general crypto on-ramp. Both serve crypto merchants broadly and neither touches this vertical by name. Read one way, sophisticated publicly-traded compliance functions have looked at the same map we just drew and declined. Read the other way, if and when a regulated lane opens in LatAm, no regional specialist currently owns the relationship.

Whitespace

If LatAm regulators follow the CVM model and carve out securities lanes, the first PSP with pre-built relationships to regulated venues, brokers and infrastructure providers wins the category by default. Positioning costs little today.

The important caveat: absence of public evidence proves nothing about confidential merchant rosters. PSP client lists are rarely fully disclosed, and this finding should be re-verified before it appears in any external material.

The playbook, in three stages.

Guardrails now, scoped exploration next, structural options held in reserve.

Stage 1 · Immediate. Lock the public posture and the internal guardrails. No brand association with Polymarket, Kalshi's global retail product, or any blocked platform. No prediction-market marketing to Brazilian, Colombian, Argentine or Peruvian audiences. The Brazilian PI declines any prediction-market operator as a merchant, categorically, and treats crypto settlement of offshore legs as prohibited ahead of Resolution 561's October 1 effective date. Any adjacent opportunity requires legal sign-off mapped to the specific entity, license and jurisdiction.

Stage 2 · Near-term. Explore only regulated counterparties: the CVM lane in Brazil (B3-listed contracts, the Kalshi–XP brokerage structure) and CFTC-regulated venues outside our consumer footprint, framed as capital-markets servicing rather than betting. Assess adjacent B2B revenue in data, market-making and infrastructure. In Mexico only, scope a compliant fiat-to-USDC on-ramp under the Fintech Law VASP perimeter, explicitly ring-fenced from prediction-market marketing and gated on the pending gambling reform.

Stage 3 · Contingent. Any European move requires MiFID II authorization analysis first; ESMA has closed retail, so only institutional authorized activity is viable. Keep European and LatAm activity in separate legal entities with no shared merchant onboarding, so nothing hypothetical in Europe can contaminate the BCB license.

Approved public line
LaFinteca is monitoring the emerging regulated event-contract space and its evolving treatment across jurisdictions. We do not and will not service platforms operating outside the regulatory perimeter in any market where we hold a license.

Four benchmarks flip the recommendation. CVM issuing a clear licensing pathway plus SPA/CMN confirming PSP servicing of regulated instruments upgrades the CVM lane from explore to pursue. Mexico's reform passing determines whether the grey zone opens or closes. The CFTC finalizing its June rule with Supreme Court affirmation of federal preemption makes the derivatives framing durable and exportable. And any LatAm country enacting a securities carve-out for event contracts creates a first-mover B2B servicing window.

Confidence & caveats.

  • Recency risk — CMN 5,298, BCB 561, the ESMA statement, the CFTC NPRM and Kalshi's Series F all landed April–July 2026. Final rules, court outcomes and the US Supreme Court question remain open. Every permitted/banned label is a snapshot as of July 15, 2026.
  • Sourcing mix — Primary regulator texts used where available (BCB, CVM/CMN, CFTC, ESMA, Senado de Chile). Block lists, deposit mechanics and grey-zone characterizations rely partly on trade press and affiliate guides.
  • Kalshi–XP structure — The Brazilian regulatory basis is asserted by the parties and has not been blessed by a Brazilian regulator. Treat as unconfirmed until CVM or SPA speaks.
  • Polymarket valuation — The $12bn round was reported as sought (Bloomberg), not closed. The ICE-implied $8–9bn figure is the last firmly documented valuation.
  • Negative findings — No public evidence of dLocal, EBANX, Nuvei or TransFi servicing this vertical does not rule out confidential relationships. Re-verify before external use.
  • Brazil's two-track boundary — The line between a prohibited event contract and a permitted economic-financial benchmark sits at CVM's discretion, and the SPA-versus-CVM jurisdiction question is explicitly unsettled per the Finance Ministry's March 2026 statement.
  • Processor enforcement — No LatAm prosecutions of payment processors are documented yet, but Peru, the Chilean bill and Brazil's October 2026 VASP-servicing ban all point at payment-level enforcement. Absence of precedent is not safety.

Guardrails now, regulated lanes next.

The legal map is split down the middle across LaFinteca's markets — banned at retail in Brazil, blocked in Colombia and Argentina, open only through licensed exchanges like B3's Kalshi–XP structure. The playbook follows that split: no brand association and no merchant onboarding for any unlicensed platform today, scoped exploration of only CVM- and CFTC-regulated counterparties next, and structural European options held in reserve until MiFID II clears. Four concrete triggers — CVM issuing a licensing pathway, Mexico's reform passing, the CFTC's June rule surviving Supreme Court review, and any LatAm securities carve-out for event contracts — are what would move this from watch to pursue.
