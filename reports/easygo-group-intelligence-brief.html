The three things to remember.

If the deck only has space for three lines about Easygo, these are the three. Everything else in this document supports them.

01

Fully founder-owned. No outside capital, ever.

Bijan Tehrani owns ~66.7% of Easygo Entertainment Pty Ltd directly. Ed Craven owns ~33.3% via Ashwood Holdings. No external investors, no VC, no PE, no IPO filing, no employee equity plan disclosed. The December 2024 A$4.78bn internal restructuring was funded by issuing new shares to the same two founders.

02

A US$4.7bn-GGR operator larger than MGM's Vegas casino business.

Stake told Forbes it generated US$4.7bn in gaming revenue in 2024 — "more than MGM reported earning from casino games at all of its Las Vegas properties." Easygo Group Holdings' FY-Jun-2025 ASIC accounts: A$970m revenue, A$257m net profit, A$5.07bn net assets, 636 staff in scope.

03

Mid-migration to fiat. No global PSP publicly named.

Craven told The Australian in March 2025 that ~70% of Stake's transactions are now in fiat. The group operates regulated entities in Brazil, Colombia, Peru, Mexico, Italy and Denmark with no incumbent global PSP publicly disclosed. The procurement window is open. This is the LaFinteca-relevant headline.

What this means for the investor map

Easygo will not appear in any iGaming funding-round database. No round to plot, no fund to credit, no exit multiple to model. In a deck about "external funds and firms that invested in iGaming companies," Easygo is the conspicuous absence.

That absence is itself the finding. It tells us crypto-margin reinvestment at scale can substitute for external capital in this category. Bet365 / Coates and Pinnacle are the closest analogues — both founder-controlled, both never publicly raised. Easygo joins that very short list at the top of the operator stack.

"The biggest movers in iGaming aren't always the ones taking outside money."

Position this as a sidebar in the deck: Tier-1 outliers — founder-funded operators at scale. One slide. Three names. The point lands in seven seconds.

Did anyone ever fund
this company?

This is the headline claim in the brief, and it deserves the work. The short answer is no — Easygo has never taken external equity, has no disclosed debt facility, and operates on reinvested operating cash. The longer answer requires walking through what would falsify that claim and showing it isn't there.

Verdict

No external capital. Founder-funded since day one.

Built on Primedice's 2013 Bitcoin-dice cash flow. Stake launched 2017 from the same capital base. Every subsequent expansion — Kick, Twist Gaming, Idealbet, Stake Denmark, the Sauber sponsorship, Maincode — financed from operating profit and founder personal wealth.

Confidence · High

Why we can say this with confidence

Three categories of evidence converge: (1) no record of a funding round in any database that tracks them, (2) no disclosure consistent with external equity in Australian regulatory filings, and (3) cash-flow math that doesn't require external capital to explain.

What would falsify it: an ASIC filing showing a third-party shareholder, a Crunchbase/PitchBook funding record, a credit facility disclosed in a counterparty filing, or a public statement of capital raised. None exist as of May 2026.

The one ambiguity worth surfacing: Tehrani publicly stated in April 2026 that the founders "personally invested close to US$1bn in Kick." That is founder personal capital flowing into a subsidiary — not external capital flowing into the group. The distinction matters.

— What we considered as counter-evidence and ruled out —

Considered · The Sauber deal

Is Stake F1 sponsorship secretly an equity round?

It is not. The Sauber arrangement is a title sponsorship — Stake pays Sauber for branding rights on the car and team. The money flows the wrong direction for an equity investment. Craven separately co-owns minority Sauber motorsport interests, but that's outbound personal investment, not inbound to Easygo.

Considered · PointsBet

Did the PointsBet stake represent outside capital?

No — direction reversed. Easygo Gaming held ~5% of PointsBet (outbound investment), then sold into MIXI's A$1.25/share takeover in August 2025 (cash exit, inbound to Easygo). This was Easygo deploying capital, not receiving it. The realised proceeds added to the group's cash position.

Considered · The hack

Did Easygo raise capital to cover the Sept 2023 US$41m Lazarus loss?

No evidence of any capital raise tied to the hack. The loss was absorbed from operating cash. Stake's GGR continued to grow (US$2.6bn 2023 → US$4.7bn 2024) and FY25 accounts show A$116m cash plus A$257m net profit — comfortably more than required to cover a one-time US$41m write-down without external help.

Considered · The Italy/Denmark deals

Were the 2024 acquisitions debt-financed?

The Idealbet acquisition (Aug 2024) and Stake Denmark deal (Jul 2024, A$7.1m) were small relative to group cash flow. The total of disclosed 2024 M&A — including the unnamed A$45.1m July deal — is comfortably absorbable from operating cash. No bridge financing, no acquisition facility, no rights issue disclosed.

Implication · for a licensed LATAM EMI

The procurement window is open, and it won't stay that way.

Easygo demonstrates that a fully founder-funded crypto-native operator can scale to A$5bn+ in net assets without raising external capital — but in the process, it has built a payments stack that is highly opaque from the outside, almost certainly procured ad-hoc, and now in the middle of a forced migration from crypto-only rails to a hybrid fiat-and-crypto architecture as the group regularises in Brazil, Colombia, Peru, Mexico, Italy, and Denmark.

The 70%-fiat figure Craven gave The Australian is the single most consequential data point in this file. Stake is rebuilding its banking and PSP layer right now, in markets where LaFinteca already holds licensure and operates flow.

The competitive constraint is not pricing or feature set. It is institutional access: Easygo procurement runs out of a small Melbourne team and prefers private commercial relationships. The strategic risk is regulatory — California sweepstakes litigation and Curaçao reform.

"Regulated LATAM EMI with single-contract Pix/SPEI/PSE coverage and provable AML/KYC for a group under active enforcement scrutiny in the U.S." — that framing matches both Easygo's current pain points and the LaFinteca stack.

What could change the recommendation.

Five active risk vectors. The first two would materially raise the friction for any payments institution touching Stake flows — they are the kill-switches on the BD case. The remaining three are watch-items.

Kill-switch · US

California AB 831 + LA City Attorney injunction

The pending bill would prohibit financial institutions, payment processors, geolocation providers and platforms from supporting sweepstakes operators. If it passes — or if the LA City Attorney secures preliminary injunction in LASC 25STCV25304 — any direct PSP relationship with Sweepsteaks Limited becomes regulatorily prohibitive in the US. Decision: avoid Sweepsteaks entity entirely.

Kill-switch · Curaçao

Curaçao reform impact on Medium Rare N.V.

Stake has migrated from the legacy Antillephone master-licence regime to direct CGA oversight (OGL/2024/1451/0918). If the reform tightens further on AML or beneficial-ownership transparency, the global flow rebases. Watch the CGA's next round of operator audits.

Watch · LATAM

Argentina & Chile entry

Both markets are logical next moves given current Argentine GGR concentration. A formal local entity in either market expands the addressable PSP wallet. Track licensure filings with each national regulator.

Watch · competition

Single LATAM-wide PSP appointment

If a Tier-1 source confirms Stake has appointed EBANX, dLocal, or any single regional PSP across multiple LATAM markets, the procurement window narrows materially. Retreat to a niche-method differentiation strategy: open-banking Pix recon, FX optimisation, settlement-speed SLAs.

Watch · brand

Reputational drift

Drake fallout, Bonnie Blue UK exit, multi-state US litigation, the Lazarus hack — none individually impair business, but cumulatively they raise reputational diligence requirements for any partner bank assessing the relationship. LaFinteca's regulated EMI status is a feature here, not a liability.

Watch · talent

Drake / sponsorship volatility

Per CasinoBeats (Aug 2025), Drake publicly called Craven a "snake" and deleted his Kick account. By Feb 2026 he was still placing Stake-branded bets. The relationship is strained, not formally terminated — but the US$100m/yr reported value is unaudited and the deal status should be treated as "soft." Not a deal-breaker for payments BD.
